## How Lexamica Actually Works (Full Breakdown)

### The Flow

1. **Lead generation / intake:** PI firms run call centers (called "intake teams") that pre-screen potential clients. The client calls in, the intake team qualifies them.
2. **Integration handoff:** Intake data pushes through an integration onto Lexamica's platform. This is automatic — not a manual entry.
3. **Rule-based matching:** A real-time deterministic rule engine matches claims to partner firms. NOT AI. NOT machine learning. Rules.
4. **Distribution:** The client hears from matched law firms directly. Lexamica is invisible to the end client.
5. **Network management:** The real work. Curating and performance-tracking the network of receiving firms.

### Why Deterministic, Not AI

Gabriel was emphatic about this. Firms want control, not suggestions:

> *"People like how deterministic it is because they want control. They don't want AI saying, well, like, 'cause item number one on this list might be the guy they've sent cases to for twenty years."*

The #1 slot in a firm's referral list isn't the "best match" algorithmically — it's the partner they've had a 20-year relationship with. AI that reorders that list based on "optimization" would destroy trust and destroy the sale.

This is a profound insight for CaseDelta: in legal, relationships and human control trump algorithmic optimization. Any AI feature that removes attorney control will face massive resistance. The winning approach is AI that does the work but lets the attorney review, override, and direct.

### What's Actually Hard

Gabriel pushed back on Camren's curiosity about the matching engine:

> *"I mean, I guess I can answer two ways. One is we don't do it. Two is it's not hard to start doing that on some level."*

The matching itself is simple. The hard part is downstream:
- Managing and curating network performance (which firms actually close referred cases?)
- Performance-based routing — dynamically adjusting distribution based on outcomes
- Onboarding a client's existing network (they bring "dozens and dozens" of partner firms)
- Making it "not fail" — operational reliability when you're handling someone's revenue stream

> *"The actual hard work is managing and curating the performance of a network of those law firms."*

Lesson for CaseDelta: the moat isn't in the AI/tech. The moat is in the operational layer — understanding how firms actually work, integrating into their real workflows, and delivering reliability.

### The "They Hate It Then Love It" Dynamic

> *"And they hate it, and then they love it because it gets better."*

Gabriel described an onboarding pattern where firms initially resist the operational changes Lexamica imposes. But once results show (better referral tracking, more revenue, fewer dropped cases), they become advocates. CaseDelta should expect the same: initial resistance to AI ("we've always done it this way"), then adoption once time savings become undeniable. Plan for a rough first 2-4 weeks with each client.

## The Hourly Billing vs. Contingency Insight (CRITICAL)

This was perhaps the most strategically important moment in the call, and it was easy to miss. Gabriel was talking about the PI business model and said:

> *"Unlike hourly billing where you don't want to necessarily — you don't wanna reduce the input of work..."*

He was explaining a fundamental structural difference:

**Hourly billing practices** (family law, estate, corporate, immigration): Revenue = hours worked x billing rate. AI that makes work faster REDUCES revenue. This creates structural resistance to efficiency tools. The attorney literally earns less if they work faster.

**Contingency practices** (PI, mass tort, workers comp): Revenue = % of settlement/verdict. AI that makes work faster = handle more cases = more revenue. Efficiency tools INCREASE revenue. Attorneys want to work faster because they can take on more cases.

> *"Because you can do both, right?"*

In contingency, you can increase output AND increase revenue. That's why PI firms are willing to pay $700/case for EvenUp — it doesn't eat into their billing, it multiplies their capacity.

**Massive implication for CaseDelta's practice area strategy:**
- If CaseDelta targets hourly-billing firms (family law, estate, immigration), the AI efficiency pitch fights against the revenue model. You're asking lawyers to make less money per case. The sell has to be framed around capacity ("take on more clients") or quality ("better outcomes"), NOT speed.
- If CaseDelta targets contingency firms (PI, workers comp), the AI efficiency pitch aligns perfectly with the revenue model. Faster = more cases = more money. But you're walking into the $500-750M capital wall.
- This is a fundamental strategic tension that CaseDelta hasn't resolved.

## Gabriel's Advice to Camren (Exhaustive, With Context)

### 1. "Deeply understanding the business problem"

When Camren asked about selling something firms haven't seen before, Gabriel's answer was immediate and simple:

> *"Deeply understanding the business problem."*

He then elaborated that PI firms are "a bunch of one or two person owned law firms" — meaning the decision-maker is the owner. You're not selling through procurement or a committee. You're selling to one person who runs the whole business. To sell to that person, you need to understand their business as well as they do.

### 2. The "One Persona" Discipline

> *"We have basically one persona, which is like the PI buyer... large enough to have distinct operational units."*

Lexamica doesn't try to serve all PI firms. They have ONE persona: a PI firm large enough (12+ people) to have a dedicated referral management function. Everyone below that cutoff is explicitly out of scope, even though "a segment that will exist someday." Gabriel chose to ignore an entire addressable market because serving it would dilute focus.

> *"Better to serve one small segment really well than to serve multiple."*
> *"At least specific enough that they're all the same."*

"All the same" is the key phrase. His firms look alike, operate alike, have the same pain, buy the same way. Homogeneity in the customer base means you can build one product, one pitch, one onboarding flow, and it works for everyone.

CaseDelta's current ICP — "1-50 attorney boutique firms across family law, PI, immigration, estate, bankruptcy, criminal defense, employment, workers comp, med mal, commercial lit, probate" — is the opposite of this. That's 11 practice areas, each with different workflows, document types, billing models, and buyer psychologies. Gabriel would say: pick ONE.

### 3. The YC Founder in Akron (Embed or Die)

When Camren asked about accelerating the feedback flywheel and mentioned "forward-deployed engineers," Gabriel told this story:

> *"There's a guy who, in our space, got some investment from Y Combinator last year — hands-free voice intake. But like the thing that I really respect is like he just said, 'Screw it. I'm gonna go live in Akron, Ohio with this law firm.'"*

And then the deeper point:

> *"Not because the case types themselves are dramatically different, but because the psychologies of the law firms are so dramatically different from each other. They just aren't the same type of business. They don't operate the same way. They don't think about things the same way — different appetites for risk, business structure."*

This is Gabriel saying: you can't learn what you need to learn by sending engineers or doing Zoom demos. You have to physically sit in a firm and watch how they work. The product differences between firm types are secondary — the PSYCHOLOGY differences are primary. Same practice area, same city, two firms can be completely different in how they think about technology, risk, and change.

Note: Camren floated the idea of "forward-deployed engineers." Gabriel's counter was one founder going himself. The subtext: at this stage, Camren needs to be the one embedded, not a team he doesn't have.

## Deep Takeaways

### 1. The "Not a Tech Company" Positioning Works at $45K ACV

Lexamica's $45K ACV comes from positioning as an outsourced business operations layer, not a software tool. Gabriel explicitly said: *"We are not a normal tech company by any stretch."* The software is the delivery mechanism — the sale is operational transformation. CaseDelta's "another seat in your firm" narrative is the same instinct. Double down on it. When you sell a tool, you get $1K/year. When you sell an outcome, you get $45K.

### 2. Volume-Based > Seat-Based for CaseDelta's ICP

Gabriel can't do seats because there are only 1-6 referral managers. CaseDelta faces the same dynamic — a 5-attorney firm might have 1-2 people touching the platform. Seat pricing caps revenue. Consider: per-case, per-matter, or tiered volume pricing that scales with firm activity, not headcount. The $500-700/case PI pricing from Eve shows law firms will accept per-matter pricing if the value is clear.

### 3. The PI Market Is a Capital War Zone

$500-750M of VC money has been dumped into PI legal tech in two years (EvenUp $3B, Eve spending $100M/yr on GTM, Finch, Supio). Gabriel explicitly flagged this when Camren mentioned exploring PI. The family law / immigration / estate beachhead is strategically safer — those practice areas have NO equivalent VC-backed juggernauts. There's no "EvenUp for family law" spending $100M to win the market.

### 4. Gabriel Validated the "Agentic AI" Thesis

His exact words: *"If the market still has a gap, it would be around agentic AI."* This is CaseDelta's core technical thesis — agents that work across tools vs. monolithic AI products that replace attorney functions. He's seeing the same gap from inside the PI world. The outsourced-output model (Eve, Supio) is funded and crowded. The agentic model (AI that assists attorneys in-workflow) is wide open.

### 5. The Contingency vs. Hourly Billing Tension Is Unresolved

Gabriel surfaced a structural issue CaseDelta hasn't fully grappled with: efficiency tools ALIGN with contingency billing (faster = more cases = more revenue) but CONFLICT with hourly billing (faster = fewer billable hours = less revenue). CaseDelta's current beachhead (family law, estate, immigration) is largely hourly-billing. The sell is harder there. Not impossible — but the framing has to be capacity ("take on more clients without hiring") not speed ("do the same work faster"). This distinction needs to be baked into every pitch.

### 6. "Roll a Dice" = Stop Exploring, Start Embedding

Gabriel's most pointed advice: the discovery phase is a luxury you can't afford. Pick ONE firm type, embed, build for them. The YC founder in Akron analogy is clear — physical presence in a firm, learning their exact workflow, building for that reality. And critically: the differences between firms aren't in case types — they're in PSYCHOLOGIES. Two family law firms in the same city can operate completely differently. You can only learn this by being there.

### 7. The FTE Anchor + Revenue Creation = Dual Pricing Unlock

Lexamica anchors against both (1) the cost of hiring FTEs to do the same work, AND (2) the revenue their platform creates by making referrals more efficient. CaseDelta currently only uses the cost anchor ("paralegal = $50-70K, CaseDelta = $6-12K"). The revenue anchor is missing: "How many more cases can you take on when doc review takes 20 minutes instead of 4 hours? What's each case worth?" If a firm can take on 3 more cases/month at $5K each, CaseDelta enables $180K/yr in new revenue. $12K/yr for $180K/yr in capacity is a 15x ROI. That's the pitch.

### 8. Homogeneity in the Customer Base Is the Scaling Prerequisite

Gabriel's "at least specific enough that they're all the same" is the most underrated advice in the conversation. If all your customers look alike, you build one product, one onboarding flow, one pitch deck, one set of case studies. If they're all different (family law vs. PI vs. immigration vs. estate), you're building 11 products, 11 pitches, 11 onboarding flows. At this stage, CaseDelta can't afford to build 11 of anything.

### 9. Relationship Is Mentorship, Not Partnership (Yet)

Gabriel is generous with time and knowledge but this is a "come back when you've done the work" relationship. He's not going to open his 800-firm network until Camren proves execution. The follow-up in ~1 month should be a concrete update: "I embedded at [firm], built [feature], here's what happened." Not another exploratory conversation. And not just "I'm still figuring out which practice area." He was clear: the price of his continued investment is Camren's execution.

### 10. "It Has to Not Fail" — Reliability Is the Enterprise Moat

Gabriel kept coming back to operational reliability. *"It has to not fail."* *"That needs to run really well, and that needs to run end to end."* When you're handling a firm's revenue stream (referrals) or case workflow (CaseDelta), any downtime or error is catastrophic. Law firms are risk-averse. One bad AI output on a real case and you lose the client forever. This is why Gabriel's platform is deterministic, not AI — because deterministic means predictable, and predictable means trustworthy. CaseDelta's AI layer needs a confidence/review mechanism. Never auto-execute on a real case without attorney review.
