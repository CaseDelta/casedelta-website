## **Meeting Summary — Camren Hall & Heather Landon**

### **Context & Camren's Pivot**

Camren opened by recapping the previous meeting, where the product was focused on AI-driven client discovery and RFP document reconciliation — a tool that felt more paralegal-facing than attorney-facing. Camren acknowledged this was too narrow and has now pivoted toward **secure, accurate AI document analysis** built for small to mid-market solo and small law firms. The security angle is central to the new direction, as it's been a major blocker for lawyers interested in AI.

---

### **Heather's Profile & Self-Assessment**

* Solo Family Law Attorney, primarily focused on **GAL (Guardian ad Litem)** work — meeting with parents, writing reports, representing children's interests.  
* She considers herself a **tech "dinosaur"** and is very candid about it. She's resistant to adopting new tools, not out of fear but because she's too busy and needs tools to be incredibly simple and intuitive.  
* She's currently in the middle of onboarding **MyCase** (a legal practice management/billing platform) and that process alone has been drawn out and painful — she's been "driving the rep crazy."  
* She recently started using **Zoom's AI meeting summary** feature and loves it because it requires just one click, then auto-generates notes and a summary. This is a meaningful indicator of the UX standard she needs.  
* She currently uses **ChatGPT** casually for personal tasks (e.g., visualizing paint colors for her house).  
* She also serves as a **Municipal Court Judge in Bonner Springs**, which adds to her already packed schedule.

---

### **Workflow Pain Points**

**Biggest friction: The financial side of divorce cases.** When representing a spouse in a contested divorce, the financial discovery process — building spreadsheets, analyzing bank statements, reconciling documents — is extremely time-consuming and something she "hates." She has structured her practice to minimize this work, focusing on GAL/child-centered cases instead. However, she mentioned she plans to bring on a new associate (her daughter, straight out of law school) who will handle more of the financial representation work.

**Document disorganization from opposing counsel** is another major pain point. She described opposing counsel frequently dumping an unlabeled mess of files into a shared Dropbox or drive — no folder structure, no correlation to the discovery requests. She was emphatic: if a tool could automatically ingest those files, rename them, and organize them into folders keyed to the specific discovery requests (e.g., "Request \#1 – Bank Statements"), that would be **extremely valuable**. Camren noted this was already in the original feature set, and Heather's reaction was enthusiastic.

**Document volume** for a contested case typically ranges from **250 to 1,000 pages**, with high-asset financial divorces on the higher end — though those are cases she actively avoids.

---

### **AI Adoption & Security Concerns in the Legal Community**

* There is **widespread fear and caution** around AI among attorneys, partly cultural and partly regulatory. In Johnson County, there is a **local court rule** requiring attorneys to certify that AI was not used in filed documents. This rule came about because pro se litigants were submitting AI-generated filings with hallucinated (nonexistent) case citations, which angered the court.  
* Heather confirmed security is not her personal blocker — it's just workflow and bandwidth. But she emphasized that for the broader attorney market, **security and compliance will be a major sales hurdle** that Camren will need to directly address.

### **Key Takeaways for Product & Sales Strategy**

The clearest signals from this meeting:

**Most compelling features for this market (in order of enthusiasm):**

1. Automatic organization of incoming discovery documents — renaming and sorting unlabeled files into structured, request-keyed folders  
2. Financial spreadsheet generation from raw documents (bank statements, etc.)  
3. Secure document analysis that attorneys can actually use without violating ethics rules or court rules

**UX imperative:** The tool needs to be extremely simple — nearly zero learning curve. Think "one-click like Zoom AI." Heather is not the target power user, but her reaction to features is a useful proxy for the least tech-comfortable segment of the market.

**Pricing sweet spot:** $300–$400/month for attorneys doing financial divorce work. Frame it as less than one billable hour. Higher price sensitivity among pure GAL/child-focused attorneys like Heather who rarely touch financial cases.

**Sales strategy:** Lead with the document organization feature, not the financial analysis. That resonated most viscerally. Security and compliance messaging will be essential to get attorneys to even engage.
