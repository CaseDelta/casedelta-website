## 1. Lawyers' Adoption of AI — Market Realities

### Ethical Constraints Are the Core Barrier

Liam opened (before the recording started, continuing into it) by explaining that law firms face a unique adoption challenge: engaging with AI tools that could jeopardize their ethical obligations would fundamentally destroy their business. Unlike other industries where data risk is a cost-benefit calculation, for lawyers it is existential. A breach of client confidentiality or an ethics violation isn't a PR problem — it's a career-ending event.

Liam's assessment is that the technology can already meet lawyers' needs. The real barrier is trust and marketing. No one in the legal market has yet convinced firms — especially small and mid-market firms — that AI tools are safe enough to use with client data.

### The "Build vs. Buy" Shift

Liam shared an important market trend: law firms are increasingly moving away from buying third-party AI tools and toward building their own internal solutions. He described this as anecdotal but consistent across his network. As a specific example, he referenced a CIO contact at a large firm who previously ran a "sleepy" IT department and is now a national speaker on legal AI. This CIO has led his firm to develop proprietary tools using their existing platforms and data rather than purchasing external products.

Liam noted a caveat that creates opportunity for Camren: the people building these internal tools are often data analysts, not deep software engineers. They may lack the technical depth to build production-grade agent infrastructure — which is exactly where CaseDelta and AgentFrame could plug in, especially as firms don't want to use raw developer tools like Claude Code directly.

### Competitor Landscape: White Shoe

Liam mentioned a company called **White Shoe** — a small, early-stage competitor positioning itself as "secure from day one" for legal AI. It's a two-person team (one lawyer, one business-development person). They were trying to raise a pre-seed round as of last summer. Liam characterized them as "super early and small" and noted that KCRise had spoken with them briefly. This is a direct competitor to be aware of, though they appear to be at an earlier stage than CaseDelta.

### Enterprise Pricing Is Shutting Out Smaller Firms

Liam confirmed what Camren has been hearing in his own market research: enterprise AI contracts for legal tools are running at $1,000+ per seat, which is completely unfeasible for small and mid-market firms. This price point validates Camren's strategy of targeting the $300–$400/month range to capture the underserved segment.

## 2. CaseDelta — Positioning & Target Market Discussion

### Camren's ICP Breakdown (as shared with Liam)

Camren described his ideal customer profile in detail:

- **Practice areas:** Family law, immigration, and estate planning are the strongest fits. These practices have consistent, document-heavy workflows — clients always submit the same types of documents, making AI automation highly valuable. Personal injury (PI) is a maybe — it can be variable depending on the case and client, sometimes document-heavy and sometimes not.
- **Lawyer age profile:** Roughly 30–55 years old — old enough to have established practices but young enough to be open to technology adoption.
- **Firm size:** 2 to 30 lawyers, potentially up to 50. Small to mid-market firms that are too small for enterprise contracts but large enough to benefit from AI tools. Larger firms (50+) tend to have the budget and IT staff to build internally.

### Pricing Strategy

Camren shared that he's been testing pricing in the $300–$400/month range per firm, which is dramatically below the $1,000+/seat enterprise pricing. He noted the tension between subscription pricing (which provides revenue predictability) and usage-based pricing. His read on the market is that document-heavy, security-conscious lawyers may prefer paying for what they actually use rather than a flat subscription — they want to feel in control of their spend. Liam acknowledged hearing similar sentiment from others in the space. This pricing model question remains open and is something Camren is interested in getting further market data on.

### The AgentFrame Flywheel

Camren explained the CaseDelta-AgentFrame relationship to Liam: CaseDelta is the legal vertical product, and AgentFrame is the underlying developer infrastructure. They're cyclical — improvements to one benefit the other. He emphasized that he's focusing on CaseDelta first to make it "absurdly good" before expanding AgentFrame to other verticals (medical, fintech, etc.). He doesn't want to spread too thin.

Liam clearly resonated with this. He acknowledged the logic of building one vertical deeply before platforming.

### Security as the Differentiator

Camren emphasized that his core differentiator is security — no PII leakage, tenant isolation, data never leaving the CaseDelta ecosystem. He positioned this as the key reason small and mid-market firms would choose CaseDelta over trying to use generic AI tools or building internally.

### Camren's Interest in Liam's Fractional GC Services

Camren expressed that he would "very much entertain the idea" of engaging Liam's consulting/fractional general counsel capability in the near future. He sees value in having someone who understands both the legal industry and the startup landscape, particularly as CaseDelta navigates security concerns, enterprise contracts, and potential fundraising. This was mentioned naturally in conversation — not as a hard ask, but as a genuine signal of interest.

## 4. Co-Founder Discussion

Camren raised the topic of potentially bringing on a co-founder, acknowledging that the solopreneur path has limits. He described his thinking as still early-stage.

### Camren's Framing

He sees two possible directions: either a lawyer with industry credibility who can be the face of the brand to law firm clients, or someone focused on sales and marketing motions. He's the technical founder, so the co-founder would complement him on the go-to-market side.

### Liam's Advice

Liam gave pointed and practical guidance:

1. **Credibility is essential.** The co-founder needs to be someone lawyers will trust. Lawyers are cynics and skeptics by nature — they want to find a reason to say no. You need someone who knows how to talk to them.

2. **Values alignment matters more than age or life stage.** Whether the person is younger or older, the most important thing is that they share Camren's values and vision.

3. **Be careful with equity.** Liam strongly cautioned against giving away too much equity too early. A lawyer with the right credentials and seniority will likely be unable to take a startup-level cash salary, which creates pressure to compensate with equity. But if things go well, Camren will already have to give up significant ownership through fundraising. Liam's implicit advice: find a creative compensation structure that preserves Camren's ownership.

4. **Look at CaseText's sales team.** Liam suggested studying who CaseText hired on the sales side as a template for the kind of person Camren should be looking for.

## 10. Strategic Insights Extracted from the Conversation

### Insight 1: The Real Barrier Is Marketing, Not Technology

Liam said it plainly: "I really think at the end of the day, it comes down to a marketing thing." The technology to serve lawyers' needs exists. The gap is that no one has convinced the market it's safe to use. This reframes CaseDelta's competitive advantage. The moat isn't primarily technical (though the architecture matters) — it's trust, positioning, and security messaging. Every piece of marketing material, every demo, every sales conversation should lead with security and compliance, not features. The winner in legal AI won't be the best technology; it will be the first platform that lawyers actually believe is safe.

### Insight 2: The PI Referral Ecosystem Is a Hidden Distribution Channel

Liam revealed a market dynamic Camren was not previously aware of: the personal injury referral market. Large PI firms cherry-pick their best cases (high value, minimal effort) and refer out everything else through platforms like LexAmica/Hazeltine. Smaller firms like Paymon's take these referrals without deep evaluation because they need the work.

This creates a specific product opportunity: AI-powered case triage for PI referral recipients. A tool that could help a solo PI attorney quickly evaluate whether a referred case is worth accepting — analyzing medical records, estimating case value, identifying red flags — would be directly monetizable. More importantly, LexAmica/Hazeltine could become a distribution partner by bundling CaseDelta's capabilities into their referral platform. This is worth exploring even if PI isn't a core ICP today.

### Insight 3: The "CIO Without Engineers" Is a New Persona for AgentFrame

Liam's anecdote about the CIO who went from running a "sleepy" IT department to leading his firm's AI strategy reveals a persona that maps directly to AgentFrame's value proposition. These CIOs are tasked with building AI tools internally but staffed with data analysts, not software engineers. They explicitly don't want to use raw developer tools like Claude Code. They need an infrastructure layer that gives their non-engineering teams the ability to deploy production agents without deep technical expertise. AgentFrame could sell directly to this persona at larger firms — a B2B sale to the firm's IT leadership, not the practicing lawyers.

### Insight 4: Legal AI Adoption Is at the Very Start of the Curve

Liam stated explicitly that the legal industry is "just at the start of the adoption curve." All lawyers want to use AI; none of them have committed to a vendor yet. This is a critical timing signal. First-mover advantage is still available in the small-to-mid-market segment. However, it also means sales cycles may be longer because Camren is educating the market, not competing for wallet share against an incumbent. The GTM strategy should account for longer conversion timelines and invest in educational content, trust-building, and proof-of-concept pilots (like the Asylum Clinic KC opportunity).

### Insight 5: Liam Is a Potential Long-Term Advisor, Not Just a Connector

The depth and quality of Liam's engagement signals someone who could be much more than a one-call contact. He has VC deal experience (sourcing, leading, and closing seed-stage investments at KCRise), legal industry expertise (4 years at Husch Blackwell), an enormous KC network, and an authentic generosity that aligns with Camren's values. Camren already expressed interest in engaging Liam's fractional GC services. Consider whether a more formal advisory or board observer relationship makes sense as CaseDelta matures — particularly given Liam's experience sitting on boards at companies like Invary, Saile, and PLOT.

### Insight 6: Kansas Tax Credits Are a Fundraising Weapon, Not Just a Perk

The Kansas Department of Commerce tax credit program isn't a nice-to-have. If out-of-state investors (e.g., from Missouri) can sell Kansas tax credits at 90 cents on the dollar in a secondary market, it effectively gives them a discount on their investment. A dollar invested in CaseDelta through this program is worth more to the investor than a dollar invested in a competing startup without such incentives. In a competitive fundraise, this is a tangible differentiator that should be featured in the pitch deck and investor conversations. The two-week application turnaround Liam mentioned means this can be activated quickly.

### Insight 7: The Build-vs-Buy Trend Creates a Two-Tier Market — and a Third Opportunity

Liam described two market segments: large firms that build internally and small firms that buy. But there's a third segment hiding in the middle — firms that want to build but lack the engineering talent to do it properly. These firms have CIOs and data analysts but not the infrastructure engineers needed to deploy secure, production-grade AI agents. This is AgentFrame's exact sweet spot. The pitch to these firms isn't "buy our product" (which triggers their build-vs-buy resistance) — it's "we'll give you the infrastructure to build your own tools, faster and more securely than you could alone." This is the picks-and-shovels positioning that Liam himself values.
